Obsidian Tutorial

3 Settings You MUST Change in Obsidian

TS
By Tech Simpld
•
Available Now
•
6 Min Read

When you first download Obsidian, it can be a pretty underwhelming experience. You’re basically looking at a blank white or dark screen, and out of the box, the app doesn't exactly behave the way you’d expect a normal note-taking app to work.

But you don't need to be a programmer or a power user to fix this. Today, we’re keeping things simple. I’m going to show you three basic settings that you should change immediately. These will instantly make Obsidian feel way more familiar, protect your vault from becoming a cluttered mess, and completely change how you use the app.

No code, no complicated technical jargon. Just the essentials. Let's get into it.

Step 1: Fixing the Paragraph Layout (Strict Line Breaks)

The first thing that frustrates almost every everyday user is how Obsidian handles paragraphs. If you type a list or two lines of text and just hit Enter once, Obsidian will look fine while you're typing. But the moment you switch to reading mode, it completely smashes your text together into one giant, ugly sentence. It’s incredibly annoying.

To fix this, click the little gear icon in the bottom left corner to open your settings. Go to the Editor tab, scroll down until you see Strict Line Breaks, and turn it off. Now, Obsidian will actually respect your spacing, meaning when you hit enter, your text stays exactly where you put it.

Step 2: Stopping the Image Clutter (Attachment Folders)

Setting number two is all about keeping your workspace clean. By default, if you copy and paste an image, a screenshot, or a PDF into one of your notes, Obsidian drops that raw file directly into your main sidebar folder. If you take a lot of visual notes, your sidebar will quickly become a chaotic mess of random image files.

The clean way to handle this is to make a dedicated folder in your sidebar and call it Attachments. Then, go back into your settings, click on Files and Links, and look for Default location for new attachments. Change this to 'In the folder specified below' and choose your new attachments folder. Now, every image you ever paste will automatically hide itself in that folder, keeping your main note list beautifully clean.

Step 3: Making the Graph View Actually Useful

Finally, let's talk about the Graph View. Everyone loves seeing those cool, interconnected dots representing their thoughts. But when you first start out, the default settings usually clump all your notes into one giant, tight ball that’s impossible to read or navigate.

To make this useful, click on the gear icon right inside the Graph View page. First, look at Groups. You can tell Obsidian to color-code your notes based on tags, so all your work notes turn blue and your personal notes turn green, for example. Next, open the Forces tab. If you turn up the Repulsion slider, it acts like a magnet pushing the dots away from each other. Suddenly, your chaotic ball of notes spreads out into a beautiful, readable map where you can actually see how your ideas connect.

Conclusion

Changing just those three simple settings takes less than two minutes, but it completely changes how friendly the app feels for everyday life. These will instantly make Obsidian feel way more familiar, protect your vault from becoming a cluttered mess, and completely change how you use the app.
